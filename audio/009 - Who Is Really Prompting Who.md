Design Principles for Generative AI Applications (2024) + Prototyping with Prompts: Emerging Approaches and Challenges in Generative AI Design for Collaborative Software Teams (2025) + When Algorithms Delegate to Humans: Exploring Human-Algorithm Interaction at Uber (2025)

In this episode we ask who is actually in charge when people and generative AI build things together, pairing three papers that each locate control somewhere other than where the surface story puts it. Justin Weisz and colleagues at IBM Research argue that generative AI hands control of the computation away from the user, and offer six design principles to give that control back. Hari Subramonyam and collaborators at Stanford and Google watch real software teams and find that, in practice, control now lives in the prompt: you design the system by talking to it. And Marta Stelmaszak, Mareike Möhlmann, and Carsten Sørensen (UMass Amherst, Bentley, and Copenhagen Business School) invert the whole question, showing that at Uber it is the algorithms that delegate work back to humans, not the other way around.

We walk through Weisz et al.'s six principles, Design Responsibly, Design for Mental Models, and Design for Appropriate Trust and Reliance, plus the three unique to generative systems, Design for Generative Variability, Design for Co-Creation, and Design for Imperfection, each paired with concrete strategies and framed around Nielsen's idea of intent-based outcome specification. We then turn to Subramonyam et al.'s notion of content-centric prototyping, drawn from thirteen sessions with thirty-nine practitioners pairing UX researchers, product managers, and engineers across the US and India, where prompt-based prototyping dissolves old role boundaries but introduces new headaches: limited model interpretability, brittle sensitivity to phrasing, and overfitting to a handful of example outputs. We close on Stelmaszak et al.'s theory of distributed delegation, collective and hybrid appraisal, distribution, and coordination, built from sixty-three Uber patents and twenty-two driver and rider interviews, distinguishing the trace data we leak from the choice data we volunteer, and arguing that claims of algorithmic autonomy are overstated because the machines cannot delegate without continuously leaning on us. Together they answer the title: nobody prompts alone.

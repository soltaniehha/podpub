Machines vs. Humans: The Impact of AI Chatbot Disclosure on Customer Purchases (2019) + AI in Service Design: A New Framework for Hybrid Human–AI Service Encounters (2026) + Generative AI at Work (2025)

In this episode we pair three studies on what happens when artificial intelligence moves to the front line of sales and service, and why simply telling customers a machine is involved can change everything. The thread is honesty. Xueming Luo, Siliang Tong, Zheng Fang, and Zhe Qu (Temple, Sichuan, and Fudan) run the first randomized field experiment on chatbot disclosure and find that an undisclosed AI sales agent sells as well as a firm's best humans, right up until it admits what it is. Marzia Mortati and Giovanna Viana Mundstock Freitas (Politecnico di Milano) give that tension a conceptual home, arguing that service design itself has to be rebuilt for hybrid human–AI encounters. And Erik Brynjolfsson, Danielle Li, and Lindsey Raymond (Stanford and MIT) show the other side of the ledger: when generative AI is deployed to support workers rather than impersonate them, it makes them measurably better.

We walk through Luo and colleagues' six-condition experiment with 6,255 customers of a fintech lender, where disclosing the chatbot's identity before the call cut purchase rates by nearly 80%, from 0.237 down to 0.048, even though voice-mining showed the disclosed bot was no less knowledgeable or empathetic. The damage is pure prejudice against machines, and it softens when disclosure comes later or when customers already have experience with AI. We then take up Mortati and Freitas's notion of Service AI, defined by hybridity and co-piloting, and their 2×2 framework spanning Human-to-Human, Human-to-AI, AI-to-Human, and AI-to-AI encounters across the frontstage and backstage, including the uncomfortable figure of Service AI as a hidden partner and the ethical question of whether customers should ever be kept blind to it. We close on Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond's study of 5,172 customer-support agents given a GPT-based assistant: a 15% average productivity gain that climbs to 30% for the least-experienced workers, with the AI diffusing the tacit know-how of top performers, accelerating novice learning, making customers more polite, and lowering attrition. It is the case for AI that visibly empowers rather than quietly deceives.

How AI Is Reshaping Physics Education (2023–2026)

In this episode we follow AI's three-year sprint through physics education — from struggling student to tutor, grader, and research collaborator — across eight papers published between 2023 and 2026. Gerd Kortemeyer (ETH Zurich) opens the arc by asking whether ChatGPT could pass his introductory physics course; it scrapes by with a 1.5 out of 4.0. By 2025 the same technology out-teaches Harvard's flagship active-learning course in a randomized controlled trial (Greg Kestin, Kelly Miller, and colleagues), grades students' written explanations at human-level accuracy (Zhongzhou Chen and Tong Wan at the University of Central Florida), and outscores German Physics Olympiad participants (Paul Tschisgale and colleagues at IPN Kiel). Around that core we set the wider frame: a 30-author Nature review of AI-driven scientific discovery (Hanchen Wang, Yoshua Bengio, Marinka Zitnik, et al.), the DSECOP community of practice building data science into the undergraduate physics curriculum (Karan Shah, Julie Butler, Mohammad Soltanieh-ha, and colleagues), a call from McNally, Yin, Soltanieh-ha, and Bahrami for an era of experiential and heuristic learning, and Matias Zaldarriaga's DESI cosmology analysis — written entirely with Claude Code — showing where research practice is already headed.

We walk through Kortemeyer's ChatGPT case study (18 of 30 on the Force Concept Inventory, plug-and-chug arithmetic errors on LON-CAPA homework, a narrow pass overall); Chen and Wan's GPT-4o grading pipeline, where chain-of-thought prompting plus five-run self-consistency reaches 70–80% agreement with human graders at roughly $5 per 100 answers; Kestin and colleagues' PS2 Pal tutor, whose scaffolded GPT-4 lessons double the learning gains of in-class active learning in 49 minutes instead of 60; and Tschisgale and colleagues' benchmark, where o1-preview averages 35.6 of 48 points against students' 25.2 — a Cohen's d of 2.28. The Nature review names the toolbox behind all of this (self-supervised learning, geometric deep learning, AlphaFold2, physics-informed AI), DSECOP answers with 21 free drop-in modules running from histograms to physics-informed neural networks solving the Schrödinger equation, and the AI & SOCIETY piece sketches the classroom that survives: AI-fluency rubrics, graph-of-thoughts assessment, and experiential learning where knowing and doing become inseparable. Zaldarriaga's paper closes the loop — an Institute for Advanced Study cosmologist using Claude Code to run every calculation and draft the manuscript, arguing DESI's evolving-dark-energy hint is a matter-density tension in disguise — and we end on the uncomfortable question: when AI can pass the exam, grade the exam, and write the paper, what exactly should a physics education teach?
